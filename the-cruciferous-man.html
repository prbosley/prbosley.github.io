The Cruciferous Man

The chef stood in the middle of his cabbage patch, hands on hips, chewing a popsicle stick slowly and forcefully with well-trained jaws. One could easily wonder whether he bought popsicles for the sole idiosyncratic habit of gnawing on the sticks.

He wore baggy denim overalls to buffer the dusty breeze, as well as dark green rubber boots to fend against the mud. His arms and torso were draped in red flannel with white borders, and the rosy hue that emanated from his leathered, weathered skin nearly matched the faded fabric. The sky was a crisp blue, the humidity a faint whisper. A cat prowled up and down the rows of cruciferous cephalons, the spheres of vegetables materializing from rich soil that looked more like ground espresso beans than ordinary dirt. Were the cabbage heads attached to equally choppable necks that lay hidden subterraneously, to a similarly shreddable and stir-friable torso buried away?

High above, a silver tube with stubby wings found its way across the landscape, over the low-lying green mountains that watched over things from the west, toward the humble bay speckled with tiny islands, and on to the distant east.

He put his hands on his hips and turned his cheek toward the breeze. He began to ponder how much longer he would need to labor out here before he could be satisfied with his daily contribution to existence. What unbiased observers would call conscientiousness or purpose, the chef felt as a burden, an external pull he was defenseless against.

His eyebrows were bristly and thick, his ears large like the index finger’s portion of a baseball mitt. He spat unabashedly onto the dusty ground, a wet, sloppy lob with a hunger in it that dribbled ever so slightly onto his chin. “Always wash your produce before eating it,” he thought to himself, proud of his wit, and then chuckled as a sparrow dove through the scene.

He had been mute ever since he could remember. Sounds would come out occasionally, but they were never more than grunts and coughs. In the absence of verbiage, however, he had developed an uncanny ability to accurately remember the sounds of others. A phonographic memory? Even now in his twilight years, he could recall his mother dressed in a firmly tied apron and donning a blue paisley bandanna to keep her long bangs out of her eyes. He was listening to detective stories on the radio, or perhaps working slowly and carefully on a coloring book while his mom diligently chopped vegetables in the adjoining kitchen. He remembered the sound of her knife as it slid through vegetative flesh, and the way she cleared the chopped pieces into a bowl with one fell swoop of the blade. Zing. That beautiful metallic scraping noise never ceased to please him. While she worked, he heard her stories about how much of a chatterbox he had been as a toddler. She pleaded for him to start talking again. His muteness, she assured him, was only a temporary after-effect of the bad man.

A mention of the bad man always came so nebulously, accompanied by some vague talk about a police search, a cabin, bear traps, animal carcasses, meat hooks. Flashes that his present mind could not distinguish between actual memories or mere tales rolling off his mother’s dear lips. Regardless, the real trauma had been repressed. Silenced. Only dark undertones remained, and always would. His mother eventually stopped referring to it as a phase and accepted his muteness as a permanent part of him.

Next, he picked up his hoe and gave the clay a whack. A little more work could bring a bit of health to someone out there: a person he might pass in the course of a day, a child crossing the road, or a mother with a fever at the pharmacy. But he’d never know for sure who was at the fork’s end of his labor unless it were those he served personally. Too many middlemen, wholesale buyers for the local supermarket chain, customers who paid by leaving a coin in a coffee tin at his roadside stand.

Night after night, his sole satisfaction came from witnessing a table of haughty customers dig into his lovely cabbage creations. He heard them crunch and chew and moan orgasmically at the flavors, but he secretly longed to spy the common folk who shredded his cabbage for simple salads with fresh cherry tomatoes and store-bought sesame dressing, or for mixing into the batter of savory homestyle okonomiyaki pancakes topped with bacon and slathered in rich red sauce. The farmer in him wished for these things, though the chef in him desired perfection which, ironically, put his food out of reach of your average Hiroshi six-pack. If only there was a way to equate a unit of work out here in the field with a unit of well-being at a humble dining room table. He desired to know because he hoped the good karma would keep them at bay.

As he pondered, a lorry cruised by, and he could hear the grinding of dirt and rock under the revolving tires. A trail of dust, which might as well have been a tornado considering the utter calmness that preceded it, followed the small flatbed vehicle. “Yeah… an hour of my time might actually buy someone a full belly, a contented little smile, maybe help them fall asleep easy tonight… even give them one of those smooth, blessed bowel movements after a constipated week.” He could smell the lingering petroleum fumes in the air even after the truck was gone. “Good energy, follow me, please,” he thought. “Please,” he begged.

He whacked the hoe again, cutting the earth, flipping it upside down into a rich brown clump at his feet, a shade darker than the sun-baked top crust surrounding it. He swung the tool once more, violently, and breathed in the cool air. He thwacked again. “Hah, who am I kidding?” he admitted in a moment of disgust. “Cabbage doin’ all the important work anyhow. No imaginary credit’s going to protect me.”

With an air of evil determination, the chef manhandled a green head from the earth, channeling his inner executioner. Changing his tune, he then cradled the spherical vegetable like a newborn babe, and as would happen to a newly minted parent, tears welled up in his eyes, though for different reasons. Conflicting wills of death and creation swirled within him like ink and cream.

He shuffled his weary feet in the direction of his home, an old-style machiya two-story two-bedroom that could be seen across the expanse of farms that blanketed this high plain. From above, the patchwork of asymmetrical farm plots resembled a chaotic board game, its squares of various sizes and shapes lacking symmetry or pre-planning.

The farmer, as he sauntered along, moored seductive thoughts of crunchy, fresh cabbage salad decorated with crisp red and yellow veg, whose colors screamed and whose juices invigorated. Swallows dipped and swooped under the bright afternoon sun. A pair of kites glided effortlessly, as if suspended by invisible heavenly strings. The dust from the dry, single-laned gravel road spewed up in the strong breeze as the weather began to shift its tone, and the particles caked to his cheeks like cosmetic foundation.

He continued along, now carrying the cabbage head under both palms the way a sailor carries a cannonball through the underdeck to be loaded and fired. He walked up the footpath from the dirt road, past some camellia bushes in full bloom, and to his front door. Moving through the threshold of the entranceway, he shuffled through the tiled anteroom and into the living room, which was littered with days-old newspapers and notebooks full of scribbles, recipes and reflections, and into the kitchen. The cutting board was always out in its rightful place, his trusty knife as well. He heaved the ball of vegetation up and onto the counter, and it landed with a thud, after which he let out a mighty sigh.

There the head sat on the maple slab, awaiting its fate. The chef ran some water and rashly rinsed the thick green outer layer the way some parents tousle their kids’ hair roughly with a towel after a bath. The odd speck of soil remained, and droplets of water hung onto the head, brave souls free-soloing a crag, expressions of surface tension, bound to gravity as they fell, one by one, collecting in pools on the hard, knife-scarred surface below.

The silver Misono chef’s knife lay perpendicular to the kitchen counter’s edge, the blade side facing toward the man, ominous in its posturing. The sound of a distant neighbor using a lawn edger could be heard faintly. The kitchen air was cold in the early spring, for the stove was off and the warming effects of the space heater in the next room couldn’t be felt this far away. The man stood there in his slippers, facing off with the pale green head, eyes darting rapaciously from the knife to the head and back again. His breaths were intentional and measured, like a prize fighter’s. But his pulse was rising.

The cabbage had been sliced, cut, and chopped in a variety of ways: an immaculate salad with fresh scallions and figs the color of bruised flesh, topped with a pristine dressing of locally sourced black vinegar and finely ground aoshiso leaves, the combination of which produced a tang which clenched the tonsils as if they had just been unlawfully seen naked, shocked and embarrassed. It contained a hint of sesame oil, hand pressed. The dish was fresh to the extreme, and cold, with a satisfactory snap to each bite. There were also cabbage rolls filled with the prefecture’s famed black pork, delectable and as proprietary as a Palo Alto search algorithm. They had first been pan-seared, then simmered in a rice wine reduction sauce whose flavor was fueled by pure, hand-harvested island brown cane sugar. Finally, there was the umami vegetarian stew. The cabbage had been blended into a fine, wet paste along with tomatoes and garlic and white enoki mushrooms, cooked in a rich konbu seaweed stock for hours on end, replaced again and again with more of the base as it boiled down to its essential flavors, a pure and plentiful gift of nature that could only be alchemized by a certain ironic blend of man’s bountiful imagination and his stubborn determination. The entire time, great shiitake saucers the size of Christmas ornaments, uncut, stewed on their own, dancing within the pool of flavor like whirling Dervishes, which were ultimately served separately, like juicy, prized tenderloins.

Footsteps could be heard on the narrow gravel footpath leading from the farmland to the lone building that doubled as a home and a restaurant. They crunched on the ground like a bored set of teeth grinding up ice cubes.

“This is really a Michelin restaurant?” asked the mother.

“Yes, mom,” insisted the son. His tone made it clear that he had to insist on many things with her.

The mother, in her sixties, wore a modest red one-piece. The son wore chinos and a soft blue button-down.

“I read in Food & Wine that the chef specializes in cabbage,” said the comely young fiancée.

“Cabbage?” questioned the father, appalled.

The fiancée’s superb beauty had a way of emphasizing the flaws of the other three. As an illustration, her porcelain skin acted like a spotlight that shone on the father’s four prominent forehead grooves which had become defining characteristics thanks to a great many years of overwrought consternation.

“It’s what makes him so curiously unique,” she affirmed in a punctuating way.

They rang the doorbell and a faceless man promptly answered. He was obviously the chef’s assistant, as he was much too ramrod stiff to be the eccentric cook that the family had come all that way to witness.

The room was austerely elegant. The dining room table and chairs, made of rich, dark cherry wood, sat in the exact center. To one side were paper shoji screen windows which contained the shadowy outlines of freely swaying bamboo trees in their translucent texture. A hanging lamp, a refractory globe that reminded one of the cracked and molten glowing earth’s core, hovered, still. A staircase that took a hard right turn after three steps ascended upward to, presumably, the chef’s sleeping quarters, leaving guests to speculate on the state of the floor above.

“I hope we get to see him,” longed the mother, referring to the now famous chef.

“I wish we were out for steak,” moaned the father.

“I’m going to stuff my face,” said the husband. “Cabbage, mushrooms, tree bark, I don’t care.”

And his fiancée snapped photo after uncouth photo of the ambiance.

“My followers are going to adore how fabulously quaint this place is,” she said.

When the first course came out, the diners clapped their hands in tiny rapid flutters, fingers vertical and taut. When the second set of dishes was being cleared, the grandfather clock in the corner struck a deep and sonorous note, having the effect of a satisfied belch. The main course brought with it a biting breeze as the kitchen door flung open, attacking the guests’ bare ankle skin with flirtatious brutality. A simple dry grassy flower drooped sadly from a thin baby blue cylindrical vase at the center of the table. Its fragility brought with it a beauty unrivaled. No one thought it overtly, but it was clear in that primal place of the mind that this flower thrived in cabbage patches.

To the disappointment of the diners, the meek chef remained in the kitchen the entire time. Occasionally they could see his small hunched back shuffling by the door frame to fetch something, a bottle of rice wine, a spare hand towel, but they never saw his face. So they imagined a face of elegant determination or one of confident knowhow, but their guesses were never right. Instead, the chef’s genius was born out of an insane fear of the very cuisine he had mastered. He worried that the cabbages would rise up against him, their tyrant like embittered peasants. This unfair treatment has gone on long enough, they would say. Not one more chop. A trip through the food processor and you will pay for it. Another blanch and I will murder you vengefully.

“Must stay busy. Knife in hand,” he thought frantically. “Must not think of the bad men lurking.”

After the diners polished off the entirety of the cabbage cuisine, the host-cum-waiter brought out espressos to signal the end of the meal. The drinks were violently strong and viscously hot. Served in sturdily manufactured transparent glasses, they sat on the table, steaming, with a peculiar excessive mass to them, like caffeinated kettlebells that seemed to communicate punishment or shame rather than satisfaction. The wind rustled outside, starting as a broom-like whisper, morphing into a groan, and peaking at a shriek. The shadows on the thin paper window grew more animated, like a sinister Middle Eastern shadow play. The hot coffee was no antidote to the goosebumps that the external ambience had produced in the guests. It surely was time to go.

The four satisfied diners stood up from their seats, patted their bellies in content, and profusely thanked the gastronomically attuned chef who had served them so wonderfully, though their thanks had to be delivered via proxy, the delivery system being his android-esque assistant. They left the restaurant just as the grandfather clock in the corner chimed nine. Their bellies were full of cabbage and pork and garlic and wild blueberries and hand-whipped cream from cows that were sleeping contentedly just down the road. As the door opened and the bell attached to it rang, their senses livened with the pleasant sound of brass on brass and the sensation of the cool air licking their pampered skin. The fiancée was amazed at how far down her blouse that night air managed to travel: right along the highway of cleavage and into the deepest crevice of her belly button, piercing her guts. The aftereffect was a constellation of goosebumps and a faintly erotic wave that washed through her.

“What a good meal,” she said. “I hope my camera did it justice.”

“Pretty good,” said the father. “But I don’t see what all that Michelin fuss is about.”

The young man put his arm around his satiated fiancée for only an instant and firmly squeezed her shoulder. Her other shoulder drew near to him and fit into his armpit like a puzzle piece. She cooed in response to his rough jostling, but he himself secretly felt as if he was squeezing a submissive child rather than a durable life partner. His inner self had a carnal desire for a more substantial woman, one with a real anchor, some meat he could hold onto.

After waving goodbye one final time to the chef’s assistant, the foursome made their way down the steep path that they had arrived on, and waddled rotundly back to the car. A few early fireflies illuminated the edge of the path next to the cypress trees. The moment was so magical that to utter a word felt like it would spoil the wonder.

Thud. They heard a sound from the hill up above. The young bride-to-be swiveled her head reflexively to see what it was. Her instincts assumed danger. Another thud sounded. Then a third. And a fourth. Soon there were too many thuds to count, dozens of thuds. Something was most definitely wrong.

The four guests turned and looked at each other, alarmed and unable to speak. Their eyes, however, communicated exceedingly well. What should we do? I’m scared. I need to know what is about to happen. They could not help but feel they were stuck in a moment that preceded a bigger one, like creatures encased in resin. A holding cell for prisoners in imminent peril. The father-in-law had been wearing a nice dark gray derby hat for the special occasion, but, in a flash, it was pulled from his head as if it had been tied to the end of a fishing line. His wife put her hands up to her face and gasped.

When he reached up to check for the hat, all he pawed were wispy clumps of hair and a balding dome full of sunspots and moles. His jaw went slack and his eyes clouded with fear. He looked at his daughter and time seemed to slow to a slimy slither. She reached her hand to her cheek and ever so slightly parted her lips in a subtle expression of resignation. That’s when one slammed into her face with a violent and punishing slap. She was instantaneously out of her father’s view, her body rolling down the hill head over heels. Gurgling whimpers could be heard.

“Cabbages!” cried the father as one slammed into his stomach, then another into his brittle knees. The fireflies no longer emanated their soft lights. The night birds no longer sang. The cabbages continued to roll and maim, relentless in their servitude to gravity and Newtonian force.

Poor mother cracked her head on the pavement, rendering her unconscious for a week, and the soon-to-be-betrothed duo were found shivering fetally in fear in the verminous tangle of underbrush beside the path. The fiancée had bitten off her tongue, which authorities suspected was fetched and dispatched by a local forest resident, possibly a weasel. Maybe a fox. The groom-to-be had wet his pants and rarely spoke from then on out. He was useless in bed and a shell of himself at work. Their marriage never took flight, its wings clipped like a captured macaw, once wild and free with endless possibility, now a feeble creature to be pitied. A minor act in the sweeping drama of existence.

Meanwhile, the chef stood peering out of a narrow window in his kitchen. The portal had the dimensions of a shoe box, though slightly narrower and longer, perhaps a shoebox for a person with misshapen feet. It was screened in, a small casement window that could be opened and closed with a circular twisting crank. No potential intruder could possibly shimmy through it, yet the chef felt vulnerable and afraid, like prey. The window was just above eye level, making it very difficult to see anything of interest out there in the nightscape. However, he felt the urge, no the need, to peer, nostrils up, intently. His brow was knit discerningly as if sensing a proximal threat.

With his demented mind, he thought, “I’ve really done it this time.” He stabbed himself cruelly with his own words, each syllable a powerful thrust of self-disfigurement. “Why do you keep doing this?” he whined in heavy frustration.

The chef paced through the house all night, floorboards creaking, periodically checking that the doors were locked, occasionally returning to the narrow window, mustering up the courage to take another peek out into the crisp, violent night. He heard footsteps, many of them. And sinister crunching sounds, the breaking apart of fresh vegetable flesh. Or perhaps those were just memories of his own shuffling slippers and of the plethora of culinary chops he had performed in his kitchen, as if they were audio tracks that had been recorded and snipped and criss-crossed and overlapped, dancing mischievously through his mind on loop in a way that seemed real. “Am I the one causing these atrocities?” he wondered, and he became sick with worry and confusion.

The attack was covered in the local gazette the following week. Having just dined at the prefecture’s lone Michelin restaurant, a truckload of cabbage heads had rolled down a steep hill and critically injured four engorged guests. The primary suspect: a local wholesaler. The likely explanation: he had purchased a large batch of cabbages from the famous chef, and had forgotten to clasp the latch on the bed of his truck. But after asking around, they declared it nothing more than bad luck. He was not charged.

The chef himself was never considered a suspect for it was a well-known fact that he was afraid of the dark and never strayed from his home during the twilight hours, a quirk of his eccentric, artisanal nature, and it would do little good to question him because it was also common knowledge that he was mute. The owner of the truck had a shaky alibi, something about a steak dinner in town, but no reliable witnesses, only drunks, could be tracked down, so the case would remain open. And rumors flew like herons and swelled like bullfrogs from that day forward. The chef’s restaurant became all the more popular, attracting visitors from afar who insisted on ending their meal with a fearful stroll down Cabbage Patch Hill.

At the end of each night in that humble culinary establishment, if only the Michelin board knew, the chef remained, trembling at that very same casement window, his bloodshot, timorous eyes and the tippy top of his wrinkled nose peering out through the thin screen. Only his breath, with the nickel smell of fear on it, permeated that weak, wiry membrane that separated the safety of his hearth from the chaos of the eerie night.
