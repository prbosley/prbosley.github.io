The Third Rat

Okay, so here I am on my favorite park bench. It’s by a little rusty playground, right next to the library. A cherry tree is the foreman of the operation, and a rickety vegetable stand leers at the slim arbor from the lot beyond. Across the street the other way is a house-cum-establishment with a sign out front that reads “Records and Spice Curry,” with a picture of a vinyl disk. I go there (to the park, not the peppery music shop) on Saturdays after work at 1:00, and by that time I’m whooped.

I sit there, slumped over, weary. People pass by and seem faster, crisper, and brighter than me, like they’re sliding through life on ice. Naturally, wallowing in my lethargy, I envy their joy and loathe my blasé state. Cherry blossoms flit through the air. A caterpillar squirms across the scene in the way they do, like they’re sticking their butt up high then flattening out, over and over again. But I don’t notice these small miracles because I’m self-absorbed. Woe is me? Should be whoa is me, though I’m blind to all that.

It’s six weeks after my dad died. I’m on my trusty bench digging into my donburi. In between bites I’d eye the pull-up bar and debate with myself, not if I’d rep out a few, but when and to what level of pain. (With me, woe is me leads to positive health outcomes.) Then I thought about the news, the hantavirus on that cruise ship, about rats and diseases, and I said to myself, in a weak attempt to ease the pain, that at least my town doesn’t have dirty old rats scurrying around. At least I have that, a rat-free existence. I scrape a leaf across the ground with my foot, chin in chest, gloomy.

A minute later I rip off ten clean, slow pull-ups. I’m learning so much from this damn simple metal bar. Few reps, explosive. Many reps, languid and smooth. If pain were points, I’d have so many, but they’d be secrets. No digital trail. Pull-ups are humbling. You have to fail to get good at them. You either do them or you don’t. As I thought about these things, I didn’t feel better about life, but worse. But then I said again to myself, at least there weren’t dirty old rats in my town. Raising a family and holding down a job might be repetitive and humdrum, but at least there weren’t rats.

At that thought, I hop on my ten-speed and hit the road. I have things to buy: blueberries, a lottery ticket, maybe a popsicle. But after only a few revolutions of the tires, a soul-shaking scene unfolds—an azure sky with broccoli-esque mountains lazing upon it, a cool breeze that whispers through my arm hair. My torpor gives way to the beauty and I allow myself to exhale, perhaps the first true exhale of the day. I wonder what happens to all that unexpelled air. Does it get released in the next breath, as if waiting its turn, or does the new air come in and bully the neglected breath deeper down, forgotten and suffering?

I’ve seen rats before. There was that rat in the Paris subway right down on the tracks. Slender. Healthy-looking. At home. I had walked an ellipse around nearly the entire city starting at Notre Dame, but by dusk, feet like anvils, I took the subway a single measly stop home, and there to greet me was that dirty rat. Couldn’t make it all the way around? it seemed to say mockingly.

Then there was the Chicagoan rat. I had been taking care of my friend’s dog that week, and behind his condo one crisp autumn night, I heard its snarl and saw the ferocity in its crimson beads—a defender of the silver rubbish tins. It would stand up to cats, to dogs, to raccoons even! And away from that rat I scurried, too.

I’ve seen rats, and I have. Two. Barely plural.

There I am, pedaling through the world, spinning, not gripping. Feeling low. The landscape is black and white. I feel like Miss Gulch—you know, before Oz—gliding through the gray. But even she knew life’s surge, evident in her infamous cackle. Any noise-making done by me now, I suppose, would come out a moan or a gurgle. But then I see it. The rat. My third. It’s hopping down the concrete being chased by a raven. Scurrying alongside a house above the gutter tiles, it looks nothing like the handsome Parisian or the bruiser from Chitown. It looks threatened and scared, like me. And that damned bird—how it swoops down in front of it, startling it, sending it 180 degrees in retreat, then taking to the air to cut it off once again. The poor thing is on the verge of a heart attack. I can’t believe my eyes. At least I don’t live somewhere where there are rats, I had just thought mere moments ago.

My brakes are on the outs, but I squeeze them tightly anyhow. A young girl across the street giggles at my janky vehicle. Not wanting to touch the dirty pursuer, I rip off my helmet and swing it in wild circles. The bird, crazed, squawks savagely and circles around. As if in slow motion, I can see what is happening. The incipient flappings of a dive bomb. Intent. Anger. Speed. And it is not going for the rat this time. It’s coming after me! At this realization, the truly crazy man comes out. I hoot and flail my helmet even more exaggeratedly. The girl looks afraid now. I stand my ground against nature’s brute, and at the very last instant, it veers off and plants itself in the cherry tree. Sometimes a helping hand is all we need to keep on scurrying, if just for a little while longer.
