The Mycobeing

They were but one of a select few species on that strange planet. Milky white, the mycobeing sprouted from the ground like shape-shifting eringi mushrooms. It could animate into anything, gaping myco-jaws with myco-incisors that sensed the movement of the waterflies, its only food in the placid alien ocean.

When it had eaten its fill of waterflies in all their inky stickiness, it could emerge from its white surface as ambulatory figures, bodies writhing intelligently to an alien rhythm, playing, dancing. It formed pyramids, obelisks, and irregular polyhedra, creativity, art.

If you studied it, you would see that it communicated. No sounds came out, but when the climate grew cold or hot, though it could withstand far more extreme temperatures, it expressed displeasure by forming loathsome sagging recesses and a plethora of angry acute angles. When the weather was fine, it looked voluptuously obtuse, sensually curvy. It had feelings and opinions.

The world had few colors, the green water of the ocean, the black waterflies, the blue algae-like plant masses that covered about as much planetary surface as the mycobeing, and the grey lumpy rocks that protruded from the glassy ocean like occipital lobes.

The waterflies ate the microscopic watercritters that flitted about. The algae made the air and ate the sun. The mycobeing ate everything. But unlike the abundant algae, waterflies, and microcritters, there was only one mycobeing. Its white mass spread over rock and under sea, taking a million forms, whichever allowed it to feed and think best.

One day, visitors from another planet arrived. They landed on the smooth, vast mycobeing, like a white leather seat stretched taut to the horizon. Emerald waves lapped the shore seductively. The visitors’ little silver saucers speckled the white land, a new color for the bright sun to reflect.

The waterflies took notice. To the visitors, the sticky, fast-approaching swarm looked like an ebony hurricane. Its shadow turned the splendid white surface a drab gray. Giant insects five times the size of their ships began dive-bombing, their pitch-hued sludge drowning the spacefarers. The flies were far more interested in the metal vehicles than the pilots. The last of their race, they were exterminated by one-track-minded diptera. They had picked the wrong planet for refuge.

Of course, the mycobeing could have easily saved them. The only thing was, the milky, all-powerful planetary organism thought the visitors didn’t taste very good.

Millions of years later, bipedal visitors landed. This time the tables had turned. Their ships were far larger and made of metal too hard and slick for the flies to consume. After a few hundred years, the flies lost interest.

The bipeds found the white land and its shifting shapes breathtaking. They bowed prostrate before the milky monoliths, dodecahedrons, and aberrant forms. White figures emerged, exact doppelgangers of the visitors. These copies slowly enveloped them. Strangely, the people did not flinch. A stoic stare remained on the mycobeing’s assumed face, but deep within, its gluttonous brain was smiling as it savored the deliciousness. Thankfully, the airtight seal muted the astronauts’ screams as the acid slowly dissolved them.

Unlike the waterflies, the mycobeing was extremely intelligent. It knew that eating too many people too quickly would mean running out of its favorite food. So it let them populate. They hunted waterflies, slept in lavish white homes that emerged from the mycobeing’s flesh, and refrained from building or polluting. In return, the mycobeing swallowed a few now and then, always on the people’s terms. It let them believe its pearly white flesh was the flesh of God himself. Their lives became devoted to it, and the mycobeing was happy to morph into whatever shapes filled them with the most awe, keeping its scrumptious visitors around for millennia to come.
